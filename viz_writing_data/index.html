Why Most Gambling Houses in Madrid
are in Working-Class Areas?


Patricia Martínez Sastre

During the Covid-19 pandemic and unsual image became recurrent on Spanish television: gambling houses wide open in Madrid, while going out to green areas remained forbidden. Distributed along the 21 city districts there are 405 gambling and betting houses, the largest number in Spain, according to the latest data by Madrid City Council.

Nevertheless, these recreational places are far from being equally distributed. Only five working-class districts bare within their neighborhoods half of those businesses. The highest discrepancy is observed between the wealthy district of Moncloa, with 3.3 casinos per 100,000 inhabitants, and the low income district of Tetuán, with over 24.7 casinos per 100,000 inhabitants. Why does this happen?

Gonzalo López Calvo has lived all his life in the district of Tetuán, where since the Gambling Law (2009) was passed the presence of betting shops skyrocketed. Today there are 39 betting shops in a district of around 157,000 residents; the highest rate per capita in the whole city. This situation is also common in neighboring areas with similar demographic and economic characteristics.

"Where can [casinos] make money?", wonders Calvo, "where there is vulnerability", says who is a member of The Regional Federation of Neighborhood Associations of Madrid (FRAVM), an organization that brings together hundreds of citizen groups and opposed the overexposure to casinos on their streets.

"Most of them are situated on commercial streets surrounded by pockets of vulnerable neighborhoods like mine", he says.

The districts of Carabanchel (47), Puente de Vallecas (43), Tetuán (39), Usera (32) and Centro (31) are the ony districts with more than 30 betting shops; a disproportionate number compared to other dictricts with a similar population density. According to a regression analysis, the prevalence of these businesses is correlated to the presence of higher migrant communities.

"From the beginning they looked for commercial hubs in lower average income areas, with large migrant populations and where families of eight or nine members live in 65 squared-meter houses", adds Calvo.

"If that's your reality, betting shops can resemble a paradise", he adds.

That is one of the reasons why this layout has been especially harmful to migrant communities from Ecuador, China, the Philipines and Rumania, according to various scholars.

Cristina Barrial y Pepe del Amo, enssayists who wrote the book "The lost bet. Gambling, city and resistance (Bellaterra, 2021)", pointed out that many migrants not only see betting centers as a place to chill -with affordable food and drinks-, but might also feel safer in front of a slot machine than in the street, specially if their status is irregular.

Other factors, according to Calvo, that have an effect on the enormous success of these businesses are a high incidence of domestic energy poverty and household crowding. Amid those living conditions, gambling centers can stand out as spacious spaces, with big televisions, live futbol and central heating.

Three of the five districts where gambling houses are most widespread -Carabanchel, Puente de Vallecas and Usera- also share the highest percentage of overcrowding, with a peak over 15%. In districts where the migrant population is significantly smaller (close to 10%), the percentage of household overcrowding drops to half, and the number of betting shops falls.
